Warm-up Riddle

There is a pond covered with lily pads. The pond began with 1 lily pad, but the patch doubles in size every day. On day 48 the entire pond is covered. How long would it take the lily ponds to cover exactly half the lake?

Your first thought was probably 24. Most people see the word "half" and automatically divide 48 in half. Our brains take these mental shortcuts to arrive at answers more quickly. If you did not rush to judgment and took some time to deliberately analyze the situation, you would eventually arrive at the correct answer, 47.

As this riddle demonstrates, critical thinking takes time and effort and is more than just "being smart." It is a deliberate, mental process of checking the quality of your own thoughts and information you receive before you accept it as true.

Of course, critical thinking is just one of the many cognitive functions we use at work each day. Before we continue, do you know how critical thinking differs from strategic thinking, creative thinking and even problem solving?

Critical Thinking

Critical thinking is the process of analyzing available facts, evidence, and arguments to reach a reasoned judgment about what is true or right.

We use critical thinking skills for more than solving riddles! As public servants, we work in an environment of ambiguity, rapid change, and high public scrutiny. From climate change and affordable housing to digital transformation and reconciliation with Indigenous peoples, our challenges demand rigorous, insightful and impartial judgment. Finding solutions to these problems requires critical thinking.

    Like Soraiya and Aaron, we often have different views than our colleagues on a range of topics and issues. Critical thinking requires you to form your own beliefs rather than adopting the inherited opinions of those around you.

    Inherited Opinion

    An inherited opinion is when you adopt someone else's opinion as your own rather than forming your own judgment.

    What's Next?

    Critical thinking requires the application of logic. We use our reasoning skills to determine whether or not we accept or reject information as true or right. Put another way -- if critical thinking were a physical task then logic is the tool used to complete the task, much like the relationship between a paint brush and the act of painting.
