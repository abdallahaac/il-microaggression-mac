Recognizing Microaggressions
Towards Indigenous Peoples

Welcome, learner!

"Microaggressions are the everyday verbal, nonverbal, and environmental slights, snubs, or insults, whether intentional or unintentional, that communicate hostile, derogatory, or negative messages to target persons based solely upon their marginalized group membership." *
This video presents a scenario and expert commentary about microaggressions towards Indigenous Peoples in the workplace.

This is Noah, a friendly and outgoing coworker appreciated by his team.

A public servant who, without realizing it,

is about to make a blunder with serious consequences.

The meeting is coming to an end when suddenly...

Oh my gosh, the news just came out.

The Supreme Court has unanimously recognized that Bill C-92 is constitutional,

which affirms the right in jurisdiction of Indigenous peoples

in matters of child and family services.

Seriously? It's about time.

Not again. I think it's time that Indigenous people get over it and move on.

A quiet unease fills the room.

Noah's comment disturbs several team members,

particularly affecting three of his Indigenous colleagues, Tania, Thierry, and Kim.

All three look at one another, surprised, jaw dropped.

Let's pause and analyze the scene with psychologist Judith Morency.

Noah's sudden statement has sent shockwaves through the room all at once

because it shows insensitivity, indifference,

and likely ignorance of the injustices faced by Indigenous peoples through colonization.

Noah's words contain many clichés but are far from harmless.

They carry significant emotional weight.

It's like saying, be quiet, don't bother us.

Solve your own problems. It's not my concern. Life goes on.

Noah's remark dismisses the suffering, assaults, and traumas

that continue to affect many Indigenous people and communities today.

Once again, Indigenous voices are silenced and made invisible.

Noah's comment is a micro-aggression that reopens old wounds,

breaks trust, and damages relationships.

Let's go back to the conversation.

Instantly, a warning bell goes off in the minds of Tania, Thierry, and Kim.

Behind this silence, what are these three Indigenous colleagues really thinking?

Tania, who is a proud Métis from Red River, is offended.

Does he even realize what he just said?

Isn't he aware of the impacts of colonization on Métis and other Indigenous peoples

and of what children have endured for generations?

My own grandmother was forced to go to a residential school.

That comment is outrageous.

Thierry, who is Inuk from Iqaluit, feels insulted.

How could Noah show such contempt?

Do I need to state suicide statistics of Inuit youth,

which are 10 times higher than the national average,

for him to understand the disparity between his world and mine?

Kim, who is Ojibwe Saulteaux from the northwest of Ontario, is deeply hurt.

Wow, I don't want to work with this team anymore.

I bet that some of my colleagues share Noah's views.

Even today, the disparities remain so significant.

41% of Indigenous youth living in major Canadian cities suffer from food insecurity.

Oh, well, what's the point? They don't want to hear it anyway.

Noah, are you aware of what you just said?

This comment is unacceptable.

Noah, when you say get over it and move on, it's super hurtful.

It gives the impression that the harm and suffering experienced

by those close to me are unimportant.

It's not just about the past, but also about how it still affects them today.

There are so many challenges ahead.

There are numerous repercussions that we, Inuit,

and also First Nations and Métis, experience in our daily lives.

Not so long ago, our cultures, languages, security, independence, beliefs,

and freedoms had been radically removed from the Indigenous way of being.

These past actions still impact everyday Indigenous lives.

More than half of all foster children in Canada are Indigenous, mainly Inuit,

even though Indigenous kids only represent less than 8% of the child population.

Noah, visibly unsettled, realizes the gravity of what he said.

I didn't mean to hurt anyone.

I didn't realize that my comment might come across as dismissive of your experiences.

I'm really sorry, and I apologize.

To me, reconciliation isn't about getting over it,

but it's about setting right those past wrongs.

It's about working together on an equal footing and having everyone,

Indigenous and non-Indigenous, on par with one another.

Naively, without considering the broader implications,

Noah brought the colonial elephant into the room and deeply affected the relationship.

With a few words, which he thought were harmless, Noah undermined reconciliation efforts.

Misconceptions and misunderstandings are hard to undo.

It's everyone's responsibility to speak up

after they hear inappropriate or misinformed comments.

Even when it may be difficult to do so, it's important to stand up and take action.

Reconciliation is a shared responsibility.

It's about renewing and maintaining a relationship of mutual respect

between Indigenous and non-Indigenous peoples in this country.

It's also about correcting the wrongs that were done

and committing to taking active steps for the actions needed for change.

Learn from Noah's blunder. Be part of the solution.

Be aware of the past, acknowledge the harm that was done

and its ongoing negative consequences.

Learn now about the true history of colonization and its impacts.

Find out about the government's commitments to reconciliation

and the rights of Indigenous peoples.

You have a role to play.

1. Model Curiosity

Ask Questions

Encourage reflection by asking open-ended questions to understand the comment better and promote discussion.

“Can you tell me more about that?”
“Help us understand what you mean?”
“What do you think informs that perspective?”

This is a flip card. Activated by pressing enter or spacebar.
2. Acknowledge the Incident

Pause and Address

Stop the conversation to directly address the issue and create a safe space for learning.

“I want to pause and address something that was just said.”

It’s important that we create a respectful space where harmful assumptions or stereotypes are avoided.

This is a flip card. Activated by pressing enter or spacebar.
3. Avoid Shaming

Stay Constructive

Focus on the comment or behavior, not the person, to avoid defensiveness. Keep the tone educational, not accusatory.

“Let’s talk about the comment and why it might not be helpful, rather than focusing on who said it.”

This is a flip card. Activated by pressing enter or spacebar.
4. Reframe the Statement

Clarify Intentions

Reword the comment to help the group engage with it in a way that promotes understanding and minimizes bias.

“Perhaps what you meant to express was..."
"Let’s explore that from a different angle to ensure everyone feels heard and respected.”

This is a flip card. Activated by pressing enter or spacebar.
5. Provide Context

Explain the Impact

Briefly explain how the comment may unintentionally perpetuate biases and why that’s problematic.

“That comment might unintentionally reinforce stereotypes. ”

It’s important to recognize that language shapes our understanding of different groups.

This is a flip card. Activated by pressing enter or spacebar.
